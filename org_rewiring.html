Executive Summary · Phase 1 — Executive Alignment & Current-State Discovery

Phase 1 begins where most transformations quietly fail: with leadership. Before a single workflow is redesigned or a single pilot is scoped, we sit with the CEO, COO, CTO, delivery heads, and functional leaders and ask a hard question — does the stated strategy actually cohere with how the organization is structured, measured, and incentivized today? We run an executive kickoff, conduct 1:1 interviews, review every operating document worth reading — org charts, SOPs, KPI dashboards, QA metrics, finance reports, security policies — and map what we hear against what we read. The goal of this phase is not to produce a PowerPoint; it is to produce shared truth, because every later phase will compound errors made here.

Executive Summary · Phase 2 — Department & Workflow Diagnostics

Phase 2 is where the engagement leaves the boardroom and enters the floor. Phase 1 gave us leadership-level truth; Phase 2 tests it against how work actually happens — not how it's documented, not how it's narrated in a stand-up, but how it moves from a client request to a shipped outcome. We interview department heads and role-level operators, run workflow walkthroughs with the people who do the work, shadow selected functions where interviews under-report friction, and map every handoff at every department boundary. Alongside the interviews we collect hard operational evidence: cycle times, backlog sizes, rework patterns, handoff delays, defect trends, utilization, project overruns, tooling fragmentation, and knowledge-access friction. The thesis that drives this phase is unambiguous: most "AI opportunities" are actually workflow-maturity problems, and naming them correctly is the difference between a pilot that lifts margin and a pilot that accelerates bad patterns.

Executive Summary · Phase 4 — Prioritization & Transformation Roadmap

Phase 4 is where a blueprint becomes a plan. The future state from Phase 3 is ambitious by design, and an ambitious future state can fail in two symmetric ways: by trying to do everything at once, or by taking the easy items first and running out of runway before the hard ones land. This phase exists to prevent both. We take the full set of redesigned workflows, score each one on business value and feasibility, sort them against each other, sequence them against their dependencies, and produce a roadmap that quarter by quarter names what ships, who owns it, what it must prove, and what it unlocks next. No entry on the roadmap exists without a named accountable executive and a measurable success criterion.

Executive Summary · Phase 5 — Pilot Design, Governance & Scaling

Phase 5 is where the roadmap meets reality. A plan that looks elegant on a slide either becomes a production system with measurable impact or it becomes an expensive experiment, and the difference is almost entirely about how the pilots are designed. This phase exists to author pilots that are production-grade from day one — not because they are scaled from day one, but because they are instrumented, governed, and evaluated the way a production system will be. Every pilot leaves Phase 5 with a signed charter that names its scope, its owner, its baseline, its target, the human approval conditions, the audit trail, the scale-up criteria, and the kill criteria. If a pilot cannot answer those questions before it starts, it does not start.

Plan & Charters — Pilot Design

How to move from assessment into controlled execution without confusing experimentation with production readiness.

Activities

  • Draft pilot charters: scope, owner, baseline metrics, target metrics, scale-up criteria.
  • Define baseline and target metrics before the pilot starts — no post-hoc ROI claims.
  • Define governance policies: data classification, model risk tier, approval gates.
  • Define AI operating standards: approved gateways, logging, evaluation hooks.
  • Define evaluation and observability: leading indicators, lagging indicators, drift monitoring.
  • Define adoption and enablement: training, incentives, feedback loops.

Pilot Charter — Worked Example

Support copilot pilot. Baselines: response-time p50/p95, first-contact resolution rate, CSAT. Escalation logic: any billing dispute, any churn-risk signal, or confidence below 0.75 routes to a human. Approvals: required for any message sent on behalf of a customer. Audit log: every auto-reply stored for 18 months. Feedback loop: weekly review with support lead, monthly with product.

Analysis

Non-negotiables

  • Explicit baselines and target metrics — written before start.
  • Escalation logic and confidence thresholds — named humans, named conditions.
  • Audit logs — full input/output capture with retention tied to data class.
  • Feedback loop — structured review cadence with product and ops.
  • Scale-up criteria — quantitative bar the pilot must clear before moving to production.
